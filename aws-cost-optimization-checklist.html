AWS Cost Optimization Checklist Checks 1–5 of 15
Compute & Storage
1
Unused EC2 Instances $50–$500/mo per instance
What to look for
Running instances with average CPU utilization below 10% over the past 14 days — a strong signal the instance is idle or massively over-provisioned.
Why it matters
EC2 instances incur hourly charges regardless of utilization. Even a single unused m5.large costs ~$70/month. Multiply that across a team and the waste compounds fast.
2
Unattached EBS Volumes $5–$50/mo per volume
What to look for
EBS volumes in "available" state — not attached to any EC2 instance. These are created when instances are terminated without deleting their storage.
Why it matters
Unattached EBS volumes incur storage charges ($0.08–$0.10/GB/month for gp2/gp3). They accumulate silently after instance terminations and are often forgotten.
3
Old EBS Snapshots (>90 days) $10–$200/mo
What to look for
EBS snapshots older than 90 days owned by your account, especially where the source volume or instance no longer exists.
Why it matters
EBS snapshots are billed at $0.05/GB/month. Hundreds of stale snapshots from automated backups or manual experiments accumulate unnoticed — until the bill arrives.
4
Idle Load Balancers $16–$40/mo per LB
What to look for
ALBs and NLBs with no healthy targets or zero request traffic over the past 7 days. Typically represent forgotten infrastructure from retired services.
Why it matters
Each ALB costs ~$16–$20/month in fixed hourly charges even with zero traffic. Idle load balancers provide no value while quietly consuming budget.
5
Unattached Elastic IPs $3–$36/mo per EIP
What to look for
Elastic IP addresses that are allocated but not associated with a running EC2 instance or NAT Gateway. AWS charges for EIPs that are not in use.
Why it matters
AWS charges ~$0.005/hour (~$3.60/month) for each unattached EIP. While small individually, organizations often accumulate many forgotten EIPs over time.

AWS Cost Optimization Checklist Checks 6–10 of 15
Right-sizing & Commitments
6
Oversized EC2 Instances 30–60% of instance cost
What to look for
Running instances with average CPU below 20% over 14 days — candidates for downsizing to a smaller instance type within the same family.
Why it matters
Right-sizing is one of the highest-ROI FinOps actions. Moving from m5.2xlarge to m5.xlarge halves compute cost with zero architecture changes required.
7
RI / Savings Plan Coverage 30–72% of compute spend
What to look for
Percentage of eligible compute spend covered by Reserved Instances or Savings Plans over the past 30 days. Low coverage = paying full On-Demand prices.
Why it matters
On-Demand pricing carries a 40–72% premium over RIs and Savings Plans for steady-state workloads. Often the single largest optimization opportunity for mature AWS environments.
8
S3 Without Lifecycle Policies 20–90% of S3 storage costs
What to look for
S3 buckets without lifecycle configurations that transition objects to cheaper storage classes (S3-IA, Glacier) or expire old objects automatically.
Why it matters
Without lifecycle policies, S3 objects accumulate indefinitely at S3 Standard pricing. S3-IA saves 45%; Glacier saves up to 90% on aged data that is rarely accessed.
9
Unused NAT Gateways $32–$100/mo per gateway
What to look for
NAT Gateways with zero outbound bytes over the past 7 days — likely leftover from decommissioned workloads in private subnets.
Why it matters
Each NAT Gateway costs $0.045/hour (~$32/month) plus $0.045/GB processed. Idle NAT Gateways deliver zero value and are commonly left running after services are retired.
10
CloudWatch Log Retention $10–$500/mo
What to look for
CloudWatch log groups with no retention policy — meaning logs are retained indefinitely. Lambda, VPC flow, and container logs grow without bound.
Why it matters
CloudWatch Logs storage costs $0.03/GB/month. High-volume log groups (Lambda, ECS) can silently accumulate hundreds of GBs with no expiry policy in place.

AWS Cost Optimization Checklist Checks 11–15 of 15
Databases, Serverless & Governance
11
Idle RDS Instances $50–$500/mo per instance
What to look for
RDS database instances with fewer than 1 average connection over 14 days. Databases with no active connections are almost certainly unused.
Why it matters
RDS instances are billed by instance hour regardless of load. An idle db.t3.medium costs ~$50/month; larger instances cost hundreds per month with zero business value.
12
Previous-Generation Instance Types 20–40% of instance cost
What to look for
EC2 instances running on previous-generation families (t1, m1, m2, m3, c1, c3, r3, i2, g2) superseded by newer, better-priced generations.
Why it matters
Previous-gen instances are often 20–40% more expensive than current-gen equivalents while delivering less performance. AWS no longer prioritizes price optimization for these types.
13
Unused Lambda Functions Indirect — log storage & ops
What to look for
Lambda functions with zero invocations over the past 30 days. These dormant functions are candidates for cleanup and operational simplification.
Why it matters
While Lambda is cheap at rest, unused functions carry overhead: associated IAM roles, CloudWatch log groups (which incur storage costs), and ongoing maintenance burden.
14
Underused DynamoDB Tables 30–80% of DynamoDB costs
What to look for
Provisioned DynamoDB tables using less than 20% of their provisioned read or write capacity over 7 days — paying for capacity they do not need.
Why it matters
Provisioned DynamoDB capacity is charged whether used or not. Switching to on-demand billing or reducing provisioned units directly aligns cost with actual usage patterns.
15
Missing Cost Allocation Tags Indirect — billing visibility
What to look for
EC2 instances, RDS databases, and S3 buckets missing common cost allocation tags: Environment, Project, Owner, CostCenter.
Why it matters
Without cost allocation tags, it is impossible to attribute cloud spend to teams, products, or cost centers. This creates billing blind spots and prevents effective FinOps governance at scale.
